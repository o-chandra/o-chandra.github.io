About Me

I'm a research scientist based in Boulder, CO working at the intersection of wildland fire science, geospatial science and applied mathematics. I work with KBR as a contractor to the USGS. My team, the Wildland Fire and Fuels Research Team, is based at the Earth Resources Observations and Science (EROS) Center in Sioux Falls, SD.

Broadly, I study how spatial dynamics affect fire behavior. Currently, my work focuses on understanding fuel treatment effectiveness in the Sierra Nevada using QUIC-Fire, a hybrid physics-based model developed by Los Alamos National Laboratory (LANL). As part of this project, I develop three-dimensional vegetation models using a range of remote sensing data. I also work on predictive modeling approaches to study fuel treatment longevity in the Southern Rockies.

As a graduate student at UNC-Chapel Hill and a graduate research assistant at LANL, I studied fundamental mathematical models of fire behavior. I was particularly interested in understanding how spatial perturbations affected the stability of fire fronts that behaved like traveling waves. This was the primary subject of my graduate thesis and corresponding publication. I have also studied buoyant flow instabilities in fire fronts and applications of topological data analysis (TDA) to terrestrial lidar scanning data.

Spatial Instabilities in Propagating Fire Fronts

My graduate work sat at the boundary of dynamical systems and wildfire science. My research studied when and why fire fronts that look initially mathematically stable break down due to small spatial perturbations. This mathematical question reflects how small inconsistencies in fuel, wind, or terrain grow into the fingering, splitting, and merging patterns seen in real fire perimeters. This work encompassed rigorous mathematical analysis of simplified fire-spread models and applied work with high-fidelity simulation tools like FIRETEC, connecting formal instability results to physically observed fire behavior.

Buoyant Flow Instabilities in Fire Fronts

I also study buoyant flow instabilities in fire fronts — how the upward, buoyancy-driven plume above a fire interacts with and destabilizes the propagating front itself, contributing to the fingering and pulsing patterns seen in real fire behavior.
